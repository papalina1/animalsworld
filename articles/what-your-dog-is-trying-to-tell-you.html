What Your Dog Is Trying to Tell You

By Animals World  |  Dog Behavior  |  8 min read

💬 A dog communicating through body language

Dogs are constantly communicating. Every wag, every ear position, every shift in posture — it all means something. The challenge is that they're speaking a language built on body signals that most of us were never taught to read. Once you start learning it, though, your relationship with your dog changes. You begin to hear what they've been saying all along.

Here's a practical guide to some of the most important things your dog's body is telling you every day.

The Tail Says More Than You Think

Most people know that a wagging tail means a happy dog — but that's an oversimplification. The position and direction of the wag both carry meaning:

  • Tail wagging high and fast — Excited, aroused, and confident. This is your dog at full enthusiasm.
  • Tail wagging loosely at mid-height — Relaxed and happy. This is the comfortable, easygoing wag you see when your dog is just content.
  • Tail held low or tucked — Submission, nervousness, or fear. A tucked tail is a clear signal that your dog feels uncomfortable or threatened.
  • Tail held stiffly upright — Alertness or potential challenge. This is a dog paying close, serious attention to something.
  • Wagging more to the right — Research has found that dogs wag more toward the right side of their body when they see someone they feel positively toward — including their owners.

Reading the Ears

Ear position varies with breed — it's easier to read on dogs with upright ears — but the basic principles apply broadly:

  • Ears forward and alert — Your dog is focused on something, curious or attentive.
  • Ears relaxed and slightly back — Calm and comfortable.
  • Ears pinned flat against the head — Fear, submission, or extreme stress.
  • One ear slightly back while the other is forward — Your dog is conflicted, uncertain, or trying to process something.

What Their Eyes Are Saying

The eyes are particularly expressive:

  • Soft, relaxed eyes with a gentle gaze — Contentment and trust. This is a dog at ease with you.
  • Wide, hard stare with tense body — Warning. A dog who stares like this at a person or animal may be about to react aggressively.
  • "Whale eye" (showing the whites) — Anxiety or discomfort. If you see the white crescent of your dog's eye, something is making them uneasy.
  • Blinking slowly — Relaxation and affection. Dogs blink more slowly when they're calm and comfortable with someone.

Body Posture and What It Means

Posture is one of the most honest communicators in a dog's repertoire:

  • Play bow (front end down, rear end up) — One of the clearest signals in dog language: "I want to play. Whatever comes next is meant in fun."
  • Rolling onto their back — Depending on context, this can be submission, comfort, or an invitation for belly rubs. A loose, relaxed body indicates the latter.
  • Leaning into you — Trust and affection. A dog who leans their weight against you is comfortable and close to you.
  • Cowering low to the ground — Fear or extreme submission. This dog is saying: I am not a threat. Please don't hurt me.
  • Weight shifted forward, body stiff — Alertness or potential aggression. This is a dog who has something in their sights.

Vocalizations Beyond Barking

Dogs have a rich vocal range that goes well beyond the bark:

  • Whimpering or whining — Discomfort, anxiety, or wanting something. Context is everything — a whine at the door is different from a whine during a thunderstorm.
  • Growling — A warning. Never punish a growl — it's your dog's way of communicating discomfort before they feel they have no choice but to escalate. A dog who is punished for growling may learn to skip the warning.
  • Howling — Communication over distance, often triggered by sounds like sirens. Some dogs howl when lonely; some breeds are simply more vocal.
  • Short, sharp bark — Alert or alarm. "There is something here you should know about."
  • Low, drawn-out grumble — Contentment. Many dogs make this sound when being petted or settling in for sleep — it's essentially a purr.

Your dog never stops talking to you. The more fluent you become in their language, the more you'll realize how much they've been saying — and how much of it is about how much they trust you and want to be understood.

Note: If your dog is showing signs of aggression or extreme anxiety, please consult a certified animal behaviorist or your veterinarian.
