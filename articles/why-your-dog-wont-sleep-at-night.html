Why Your Dog Won't Sleep at Night

By Animals World  |  Dog Health  |  7 min read

🌙 A restless dog unable to settle at night

When your dog paces, whines, scratches, or wanders all night, everyone suffers — including your dog. Nighttime restlessness is one of the more common complaints among dog owners, and it has several possible causes. Some are behavioral and easily addressed; others are medical and need veterinary attention. Here's how to work through the most likely explanations.

Insufficient Exercise During the Day

The most common reason healthy, younger dogs won't settle at night is simple: they still have energy. Dogs need physical exercise proportional to their breed, age, and size — and many don't get nearly enough. A Border Collie or Labrador Retriever, for example, often needs 1–2 hours of vigorous activity per day. A short daily walk isn't always sufficient.

If your dog is pacing, playing, or demanding attention late at night, experiment with adding a longer, more vigorous exercise session in the late afternoon or early evening. In most cases, a well-exercised dog is a dog who sleeps.

Anxiety and Noise Sensitivity

Nighttime is quieter in one sense — but it can amplify sounds that go unnoticed during the day. The creak of the house settling, distant traffic, a neighbor's car, a dog barking a few streets away. For dogs with noise sensitivity or anxiety, these sounds can be enough to trigger restlessness.

A white noise machine near your dog's sleeping area can help mask triggering sounds. Calming aids — from supplements to prescription medications — may also be appropriate for dogs with significant nighttime anxiety. See: How to Calm an Anxious Dog.

Pain or Physical Discomfort

A dog who can't get comfortable — shifting positions repeatedly, getting up and lying back down, circling before settling but never quite doing so — may be in pain. Joint pain from arthritis, abdominal discomfort, or any other physical source of pain is worse at rest because there's no activity to distract from it.

If your dog is older, has recently started sleeping restlessly, or seems stiff or uncomfortable when they move, pain is a likely factor. A veterinary evaluation can identify whether this is the cause and what to do about it.

Canine Cognitive Dysfunction

In senior dogs, nighttime restlessness and disorientation is one of the classic signs of Canine Cognitive Dysfunction — a condition similar to dementia. Dogs with CCD often have their sleep-wake cycles disrupted: they sleep more during the day and become restless or even distressed at night. They may wander, vocalize, appear confused about where they are, or seem unable to recognize familiar surroundings in the dark.

If your older dog has recently developed nighttime restlessness alongside other behavioral changes, CCD is worth discussing with your veterinarian. Supplements, dietary adjustments, and medications can help manage the condition. See also: Why Senior Dogs Become More Attached.

Needing to Go Outside

Sometimes the answer is the most literal one: your dog needs a bathroom break and doesn't know how else to tell you. If your dog is waking you at a consistent time each night, they may simply need an additional late-night or early-morning outing. This is especially common in puppies, senior dogs, dogs with urinary issues, or dogs on medications that affect urination frequency.

Environmental Issues

Temperature, light, and comfort all affect sleep quality in dogs just as they do in humans. A dog who is too hot, too cold, or sleeping on an uncomfortable surface may not be able to settle. Ensure their sleeping area is appropriately warm (not cold floors in winter, not hot areas near heating vents), has comfortable bedding, and is reasonably dark at night.

For older dogs especially, an orthopedic dog bed can make a significant difference in sleep quality. See: Best Dog Beds for Older Dogs.

Most cases of nighttime restlessness in dogs have a clear cause and a workable solution. Start with exercise and environment, and if the problem persists or is accompanied by other symptoms, bring your vet into the conversation. Your sleep matters — and so does your dog's.

Note: Sudden nighttime restlessness in a dog who previously slept well may indicate a medical issue — particularly in senior dogs. A vet visit is recommended.
