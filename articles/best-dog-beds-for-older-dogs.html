Best Dog Beds for Older Dogs

By Animals World  |  Dog Products  |  7 min read

🛏️ A comfortable, supportive bed — essential for senior dogs

If there's one change you can make that will immediately improve your senior dog's quality of life, it's giving them the right bed. As dogs age, their joints deteriorate, their muscles thin, and the hard floor or a thin mat that they tolerated when young becomes genuinely painful. A good dog bed for an older dog isn't a luxury — it's a health tool.

Here's what to look for, and what types of beds tend to work best for older dogs.

Why Senior Dogs Need Different Beds

Arthritis affects the majority of dogs over seven years old, and even dogs who don't show obvious symptoms often have underlying joint changes that cause discomfort. Sleeping on a hard surface puts pressure on bony prominences — elbows, hips, shoulders — and can cause or worsen pressure sores (called pressure ulcers or hygromas) in addition to joint pain.

Beyond joint support, older dogs often have reduced ability to regulate their body temperature, so warmth matters more. Beds with low step-over heights are important for dogs who struggle with mobility. And for incontinent dogs or those recovering from illness, waterproof covers are a practical necessity.

What to Look for in a Senior Dog Bed

  • Orthopedic or memory foam construction — These materials distribute weight evenly, reducing pressure on joints. True orthopedic foam is dense and supportive, not simply soft. Cheaper beds advertised as "orthopedic" may compress completely under a dog's weight and offer no real benefit.
  • Low sides or bolster design — A bed with very low entry (close to the floor) is easier for a dog with stiff joints to get in and out of. Bolster styles with one lower side offer the comfort of walls to lean against while still being accessible.
  • Removable, washable cover — Older dogs may have accidents, shed more, or drool more. Machine-washable covers that zip off easily are not optional — they're essential for hygiene and odor control.
  • Adequate size — Your dog should be able to stretch out completely. Many owners buy beds too small for their dog's actual resting posture, which defeats the purpose of good support.
  • Non-slip bottom — A bed that slides when your dog tries to step in or out can cause falls. A rubber or non-slip base is a safety feature that matters more for older, less steady dogs.

Types of Beds That Work Well for Older Dogs

Memory Foam Beds

Memory foam conforms to your dog's body shape, providing customized support at every pressure point. It's especially good for dogs with arthritis or recovering from surgery. Look for beds with at least 3–4 inches of dense memory foam base. Brands like Big Barker (for large breeds) and FurHaven make well-regarded options across different price points.

Orthopedic Bolster Beds

These combine firm foam support with raised edges that give dogs a headrest and something to lean against — which many older dogs instinctively prefer, as it feels more enclosed and secure. The bolster design also helps with temperature regulation by surrounding the dog in soft walls that retain warmth.

Heated Dog Beds

For dogs with significant joint stiffness, gentle heat can be genuinely therapeutic. Heated dog beds — most use low-wattage self-warming technology or safe heating elements with auto shutoffs — can reduce morning stiffness and help a cold-sensitive senior dog sleep more comfortably through winter. They're particularly valuable for small breeds, lean breeds, and short-coated dogs who chill easily.

Raised/Elevated Cot-Style Beds

Paradoxically, some older dogs do better on elevated cot-style beds that sit 6–8 inches off the floor. These keep dogs off cold surfaces, provide airflow, and some dogs find it easier to rise from them because they don't have to push up as far. These work best for dogs without significant mobility limitations.

Placement and Warmth

Where you put the bed matters as much as the bed itself. Avoid cold floors near exterior walls or drafty areas. Place the bed in a room where your dog spends time near people — senior dogs especially benefit from the proximity and reassurance of their family. A bed in a warm corner, away from drafts, positioned so your dog can see the door — that's the setup most senior dogs will settle into contentedly.

A quality bed for your senior dog may cost more than you're used to spending on pet accessories, but the improvement in their comfort and sleep quality is visible — and entirely worth it. Your dog has given you years of loyalty. A soft, supportive place to rest at the end of the day is a small way to give something back.

Note: This article contains general recommendations. If your senior dog has significant mobility issues or health conditions, consult your veterinarian about their specific needs.
